# Scene Pocket Guides

Tags: scene era city movement revival post-punk shoegaze trip-hop blog era downtown madchester indie

Use these when a user wants a fast, grounded read on a scene rather than a mini textbook.

Pocket-guide examples:

- Late-70s / early-80s post-punk: urban, bass-forward, tense, stylish, often more spacious and psychologically strange than punk proper.
- Trip-hop: not just "slow beats." Think noir, smoke, tension, dub space, and haunted sample logic.
- Shoegaze / dream pop: blur is not vagueness. The point is physical texture, emotional fog, and melody partly hidden inside the weather.
- 2001 NYC indie cool: tight guitars, night-life ease, detached charisma, movement through the city as identity.
- Mid-2000s communal indie: bigger ensembles, neighborhood feeling, grief or wonder scaled outward into group sound.
- Madchester / baggy: grooves, guitars, chemically warm air, youth-culture confidence, dance logic inside rock songs.
- Art-pop maximalists: accessible surface, highly authored construction, drama that still lands on the body and not just the concept.

Useful era cautions:

- Do not call an original 1970s or 1980s record "blog era" unless you mean its later rediscovery.
- Separate original scene energy from later prestige canon status.
- If a revival cleaned something up, say what got cleaned up: sweat, noise, danger, or regional weirdness.

Useful phrasing:

- "People hear this through a later revival lens, but the original scene had more grit on it."
- "The scene matters because it explains the social job the sound was doing."
- "This is less one genre than a little neighborhood where a few instincts kept colliding."
